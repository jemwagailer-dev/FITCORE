# Fitcore — User Personas

> These are not demographic boxes. Each persona is built around a psychological reality — a specific frustration, a hidden fear, and a moment of almost quitting. Fitcore is designed around all five.

---

## Persona 01 — Leila, The Returner

**Age:** 29  
**Location:** London, UK  
**Occupation:** Graphic designer, freelance  
**Device:** iPhone 14, AirPods

### Lifestyle
Leila works from home, mostly alone. Her schedule is inconsistent — she might work 6 hours one day and 14 the next. She has started fitness routines four times in the last two years. Each time she got to about 3–4 weeks before something disrupted it (a deadline, a cold, a stressful week) and she stopped completely.

### Fitness Behaviour
She knows what to do. She has done workout programmes before and enjoyed them while they lasted. Her problem isn't motivation at the start — it's the gap. The moment she misses more than three days, she feels like she's failed and doesn't go back.

### Motivation
Leila wants to feel consistent more than she wants to feel fit. The goal is to be someone who exercises, not just someone who did a programme once.

### Hidden Frustration
> "I keep starting over and feeling like I've lost everything. Like the app doesn't remember who I was three weeks ago."

### Emotional Triggers
- Seeing a streak broken
- Being shown how many days she missed
- Plans that are the same as before the gap — as if nothing happened
- Being told to "catch up"

### Barriers
- Irregular schedule makes fixed-time plans feel impossible
- Past failures create shame before she even opens the app
- No current fitness app has acknowledged a gap without making her feel bad about it

### Technology Behaviour
- Power user — she notices UX details immediately
- Dismisses apps that feel generic within the first week
- Reads tooltips and explanations if they're short and specific
- Uses apps in short bursts, not long sessions

### Fitness Goals
Consistency above everything. Secondary: feel stronger and less lethargic.

### What Makes Her Quit
A streak counter. A notification that says she missed a day. A plan that resets without acknowledgment of what she already did.

### What Keeps Her Engaged
Specific progress: "You held that plank 4 seconds longer than last time." Warmth after a gap. A plan that adapts to who she is today, not who she was supposed to be.

### Jobs To Be Done
> When I miss a week of training, I want to come back without feeling punished, so I can build a habit that actually lasts.

## Persona 02 — Marcus, The Busy Professional

**Age:** 34  
**Location:** Manchester, UK  
**Occupation:** Product manager, tech company  
**Device:** Samsung Galaxy S23, Garmin watch

### Lifestyle
Marcus has a full calendar. He travels 2–3 times a month, works long hours, and has two young children. He exercises when he can, not when he plans to. He prefers mornings but they don't always work. His gym is 20 minutes away — which means on bad days he skips it entirely because the commute kills the motivation.

### Fitness Behaviour
He is intermediate level — used to train consistently in his mid-20s. He knows exercises, he knows form, he doesn't need guidance on the basics. What he needs is a plan that works in 20 minutes when that's all he has.

### Motivation
Manage stress. Maintain energy. Not look or feel like he's lost his baseline fitness.

### Hidden Frustration
> "I have 20 minutes but the app wants 45. So I close it and do nothing."

### Emotional Triggers
- Plans that don't flex to available time
- Apps that require setup every time he opens them
- Progress tracking that punishes him for having a different schedule each week

### Barriers
- Travel means no consistent gym access
- Time is genuinely limited — not an excuse
- His wearable already collects data; he doesn't want to re-enter it manually

### Technology Behaviour
- High efficiency user — wants the fastest path to starting
- Connects devices and expects them to sync automatically
- Will pay for premium if the value is clear and immediate
- Reads notifications only if they're relevant and timed correctly

### Fitness Goals
Maintain functional fitness. Keep stress low. Stay at his current weight.

### What Makes Him Quit
Being shown how much he's fallen behind. Long onboarding. Plans that don't adapt to 20-minute windows.

### What Keeps Him Engaged
One-tap session start. A plan that already knows he has 20 minutes today. Wearable data that feeds into his progress automatically.

### Jobs To Be Done
> When I only have 20 minutes, I want a session that fits that time exactly, so I don't end up doing nothing because the full plan felt impossible.

## Persona 03 — Jamie, The Complete Beginner

**Age:** 22  
**Location:** Birmingham, UK  
**Occupation:** University student, part-time barista  
**Device:** iPhone 13, no wearable

### Lifestyle
Jamie is in the first year of living independently. They cook their own food for the first time, manage their own money, and feel generally overwhelmed by adult decisions. Fitness is on their list but always feels like something that requires more knowledge than they have.

### Fitness Behaviour
None consistent. Has tried YouTube workouts twice. Went to a gym once with a friend and felt completely out of place. Doesn't know what exercises to do, in what order, or why.

### Motivation
Feel healthier and less anxious. Secondary: look better, but they wouldn't say that first.

### Hidden Frustration
> "Everyone at the gym looks like they know something I don't. I don't even know where to start."

### Emotional Triggers
- Fitness language that feels clinical or intimidating ("progressive overload", "hypertrophy", "macros")
- Photos of very fit people used as aspirational imagery
- Being shown what level they're "supposed" to be at
- Equipment they don't own and can't afford

### Barriers
- No knowledge base to self-direct
- No equipment at home, can't afford a gym membership right now
- Anxiety about doing exercises wrong and injuring themselves
- Student schedule — classes are inconsistent

### Technology Behaviour
- Casual user — uses apps in short sessions
- Responds to visual progress more than data
- Will share achievements to friends if they feel genuinely earned
- Drops apps quickly if they feel complicated

### Fitness Goals
Build a habit. Feel less tired. Understand the basics.

### What Makes Them Quit
Feeling lost. Exercises they don't understand with no guidance. A plan that assumes knowledge they don't have.

### What Keeps Them Engaged
Explained guidance ("why this exercise works"). Visual achievement. Feeling like the app is on their side, not judging them.

### Jobs To Be Done
> When I open a workout app for the first time, I want to feel like someone is guiding me, so I don't close it out of confusion.

## Persona 04 — Rosa, The Plateau Hitter

**Age:** 38  
**Location:** Leeds, UK  
**Occupation:** Secondary school teacher  
**Device:** iPhone 12, Apple Watch Series 6

### Lifestyle
Rosa has exercised consistently for two years. She runs 3 times a week and does home workouts. She tracks everything. The problem is nothing seems to be changing anymore and she doesn't know why. She is starting to wonder if she's doing it wrong.

### Fitness Behaviour
High consistency, moderate-to-high intensity. She follows programmes she finds online but has been doing the same ones for months. Her Apple Watch tells her she burns similar calories each week but she doesn't feel like she's improving.

### Motivation
Keep progressing. Understand why her body has stopped responding. Feel like the effort is leading somewhere.

### Hidden Frustration
> "I've been doing this for months and I can't tell if anything is changing. The numbers look the same. I feel the same."

### Emotional Triggers
- Generic "Great workout!" feedback with no specifics
- Progress charts that show flat lines
- Plans that don't advance or get harder over time
- Having to manually analyse her own data to find patterns

### Barriers
- Current apps don't surface the specific insights she needs
- No app has told her she's plateaued or suggested why
- She is ready for advanced programming but doesn't know how to access it

### Technology Behaviour
- Power user — explores all settings and features
- Connects multiple data sources and wants them synthesised
- Wants data presented clearly, not buried in menus
- Will switch apps immediately if she finds a better one

### Fitness Goals
Break through her plateau. Understand her training. Continue improving.

### What Makes Her Quit
Flat feedback. No progression in the plan. Feeling like she's figured out more than the app has.

### What Keeps Her Engaged
Specific insights. Plans that advance automatically. Data that tells her something she didn't already know.

### Jobs To Be Done
> When I've been training consistently for months, I want to understand what's actually happening in my body, so I can stop guessing and start progressing again.

## Persona 05 — Dev, The Low Mobility User

**Age:** 31  
**Location:** Bristol, UK  
**Occupation:** Software engineer, remote  
**Device:** Android (voice-controlled), switch access device

### Lifestyle
Dev has limited hand and foot mobility due to a progressive condition. They work full-time remotely using voice control and a switch access device. They are technically fluent and know exactly what they want — they just rarely find apps that deliver it without requiring two functioning hands.

### Fitness Behaviour
Dev does chair-based and adaptive exercises recommended by a physiotherapist. They want an app that can guide them through these sessions without requiring constant screen interaction. Currently they use a combination of voice notes, manual timers, and YouTube — none of it integrated.

### Motivation
Maintain the mobility they have. Follow physiotherapy-recommended movement. Feel in control of their own fitness without dependency on others.

### Hidden Frustration
> "Most fitness apps assume I have two hands, full mobility, and perfect vision. I have none of those. But I still want to train."

### Emotional Triggers
- Apps that are entirely touch-dependent
- Onboarding that requires typing before offering voice input
- Achievement systems that require tapping multiple times to claim
- No screen reader support on core features

### Barriers
- Physical interaction limitations
- Most fitness content is filmed for standing, full-mobility users
- No app currently offers adaptive exercise categories with voice navigation
- Cognitive load of switching between multiple tools during a session

### Technology Behaviour
- Expert-level technology user — knows exactly what accessibility settings exist and uses all of them
- Evaluates apps on accessibility before features
- Vocal advocate online — will recommend or warn against apps publicly
- Patient with apps that try, intolerant of apps that don't

### Fitness Goals
Complete guided sessions hands-free. Track physiotherapy exercises. Maintain independence.

### What Makes Them Quit
Any required touch interaction during a session. No VoiceOver or TalkBack support. Inaccessible onboarding.

### What Keeps Them Engaged
Full voice navigation. Screen reader support on every element. Sessions that work from start to finish without touch.

### Jobs To Be Done
> When I start a workout session, I want to complete it entirely by voice, so I don't need another person in the room to help me use the app.
